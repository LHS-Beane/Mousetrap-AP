1. Big Picture

In Unit 3, we switch from “forces cause acceleration” to “energy explains what’s possible.” You should be able to:

  • Use kinetic, potential, and thermal energy to analyze a system.
  • Connect work to changes in energy and to the area under a force–displacement graph.
  • Apply conservation of energy to explain motion without doing full-blown Newton’s law calculations.
  • Explain real situations (ramps, springs, roller coasters, people climbing stairs) in terms of energy transfers.
  • Compute and compare power as the rate of doing work or the rate of energy transfer.

Reminder: Always start by defining the system and listing energy stores (kinetic, gravitational, elastic, etc.) and transfers (work, heating).

2. Translational Kinetic Energy (3.1)

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion of the center of mass of an object or system.

Key Ideas

  • Translational kinetic energy (for straight-line motion) is $$K = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2.$$
  • $m$ is mass; $v$ is speed (not velocity vector).
  • Doubling speed $\Rightarrow$ kinetic energy becomes 4 times bigger.
  • Kinetic energy is a scalar (no direction).

Common Mistakes

  • Thinking “double speed → double $K$.” Remember $K \propto v^2$, not $v$.
  • Forgetting that all kinetic energy is $0$ when an object is momentarily at rest, even if it’s about to move.
  • Mixing up speed and velocity. A negative velocity still gives a positive $K$.

Quick Check

A car increases its speed from 10 m/s to 20 m/s. By what factor does its kinetic energy change?

Answer: The speed doubles, so $v^2$ becomes 4 times larger. Kinetic energy increases by a factor of 4.

3. Work (3.2)

Work is a way that energy gets transferred into or out of a system when a force acts through a displacement.

Definitions & Equations

  • For a constant force in 1D: $$W = Fd \cos\theta,$$ where $\theta$ is the angle between the force and displacement.
  • For a varying force along $x$: $$W = \text{area under the } F\text{ vs }x\text{ graph}.$$
  • If work done on a system is positive, it gains energy; if work done by a system is positive, it loses energy.

Interpreting Sign

  • $W > 0$: force component is in the same direction as motion (speeding up or energy increase).
  • $W < 0$: force component is opposite motion (slowing down or energy removed).
  • If force is perpendicular to motion ($\theta = 90^\circ$), then $W = 0$.

Quick Check – Graph

A block is pulled along a rough surface. The horizontal pulling force increases linearly from 0 N to 10 N as the block moves from $x = 0$ m to $x = 2$ m.

What is the work done by the pulling force on the block over this interval?

Think of a triangle under the $F$ vs $x$ graph.
Base = 2 m, height = 10 N, so $$W = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot (2\ \text{m}) \cdot (10\ \text{N}) = 10\ \text{J}.$$

4. Potential Energy (3.3)

Potential energy is stored in interactions (like gravity or springs) and can be converted into kinetic or other forms.

Gravitational Potential Energy

  • Near Earth’s surface: $$U_g = mgh,$$ where $h$ is vertical height relative to a chosen zero level.
  • The zero of $U_g$ is chosen for convenience and does not affect physics as long as you’re consistent.
  • Raising an object increases $U_g$; lowering it decreases $U_g$.

Elastic Potential Energy

  • For an ideal spring: $$U_s = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2,$$ where $x$ is stretch/compression from equilibrium.
  • Graph of $U_s$ vs $x$ is a parabola; the slope gives the spring force: $F = -kx$.
  • More stretch/compression $\Rightarrow$ rapidly increasing stored energy.

Quick Check – Gravitational PE

A 2.0 kg object is lifted from a table (chosen as $h = 0$) to a shelf 1.5 m above the table. How much gravitational potential energy does it gain?

$$\Delta U_g = mgh = (2.0\ \text{kg})(9.8\ \text{m/s}^2)(1.5\ \text{m}) \approx 29\ \text{J}.$$

5. Conservation of Energy (3.4)

When only conservative forces (gravity, ideal springs) do work, the total mechanical energy of a system is constant.

Energy Equation

For a system with gravitational and elastic potential energies:

$$K_i + U_{g,i} + U_{s,i} = K_f + U_{g,f} + U_{s,f}$$

If nonconservative work (like friction) is present: $$K_i + U_i + W_\text{nc} = K_f + U_f.$$

$W_\text{nc}$ is negative if nonconservative forces remove mechanical energy (like friction).

Conceptual Rules

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.
  • Mechanical energy is conserved only when nonconservative work is zero.
  • Friction doesn’t “destroy” energy; it converts mechanical energy into thermal energy.

Quick Check – Energy Conservation

A frictionless roller coaster car of mass 500 kg starts from rest at the top of a hill, 20 m above the lowest point of the track. What is its speed at the bottom?

Energy conservation: $$K_i + U_{g,i} = K_f + U_{g,f}$$ $$0 + mgh = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + 0$$ $$gh = \tfrac{1}{2}v^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(20)} \approx 20\ \text{m/s}.$$

6. Power (3.5)

Power is how fast work is done or how fast energy is transferred.

Equations

  • Average power: $$P_\text{avg} = \frac{W}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t}.$$
  • Instantaneous power (constant force, 1D): $$P = F v \cos\theta.$$
  • SI unit: watt (W), where $1\ \text{W} = 1\ \text{J/s}$.

Interpretation

  • Two people doing the same work: the one who does it faster has more power.
  • At constant speed, higher force or higher speed both increase power.
  • Real devices (motors, humans, pumps) are often limited by power, not just force.

Quick Check – Stair Climb

A 70 kg student runs up a vertical height of 3.0 m in 4.0 s. Estimate the student’s average power output.

First find the work against gravity: $$W = mgh = (70)(9.8)(3.0) \approx 2.1 \times 10^3\ \text{J}.$$ Then $$P_\text{avg} = \frac{W}{\Delta t} \approx \frac{2.1 \times 10^3\ \text{J}}{4.0\ \text{s}} \approx 5.3 \times 10^2\ \text{W}.$$

7. End-of-Unit Self-Check

Use these to decide if you’re ready for a quiz/test on Unit 3.

  1. Can you explain the difference between:
    • force vs work,
    • energy vs power,
    • gravitational vs elastic potential energy?
  2. Can you read a force–displacement graph and calculate the work from the area?
  3. Can you use energy conservation to find speeds at different heights on ramps/roller coasters?
  4. Can you decide whether mechanical energy is conserved in a situation and justify your choice?
  5. Can you estimate power for everyday tasks (climbing stairs, lifting an object, etc.)?

If you’re stuck on any of these, go back to the relevant section above and rework the Quick Check problems without looking at the answers first.
