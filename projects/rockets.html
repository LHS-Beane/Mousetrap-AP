The objective of this lab is to build a working rocket out of a 2.0 L bottle. The rocket must be capable of carrying a fragile payload (an egg) and achieving significant altitude.

Success Criteria

  • Target Height: The rocket needs to reach a height of at least 45.0 meters.
  • Payload Safety: The rocket must contain an egg inside during launch without cracking it.
  • Propulsion: Rockets are filled with water and pressurized air (~115 psi) to force projectile motion.

Core Physics Principle

The operation of the rocket relies on Newton's Third Law of Motion: Actions trigger equal and opposite reactions. As pressurized water is forced out of the nozzle (Action), the rocket is propelled upward (Reaction).

Construction & Materials

Required Materials

  • Two 2-liter plastic bottles (one for pressure chamber, one for nose cone/payload).
  • Duct Tape.
  • Cardboard (for fins/stability).
  • Packaging foam (for egg protection).

Design Considerations

  1. The Engine: The bottom bottle remains unaltered to serve as the pressure vessel.
  2. The Payload: The top section should contain a hatch or "cubby" filled with foam to secure the egg.
  3. Stability: Attach fins (e.g., 3 cardboard wings) near the bottom to ensure balance and straight flight.
  4. Center of Mass: A weighted cone-shaped top helps the rocket land on its nose rather than its side, which aids in aerodynamic stability.
  5. Fuel: Rockets are typically filled with ~750ml of water (approx 1/3 of the bottle).

AP Report Structure

Your report must document the engineering process and the physical analysis of the flight.

Required Sections

  1. Title Page: Student Name, Partner Name, Date, Title.
  2. Introduction: State the objective and discuss the theory (Newton's Laws).
  3. Procedure: Detail the design choices, materials used, and construction methods.
  4. Data: Record flight metrics. You must include a table with:
    • Trial Number
    • Altimeter Reading (if available)
    • Hang Time (measured by stopwatch)
    • Thrust Time (approx 0.2s - 0.3s)
  5. Calculations: See the "Math & Hints" tab for required derivations.
  6. Results & Discussion: Discuss max height achieved, egg survival, and sources of error (e.g., thin protective layers).

Physics Analysis Hints

You must show equations, work, and answers for the following variables. Do not just copy numbers; derive them.

1. Max Height (y)

Goal: Determine how high the rocket went based on hang time.

  • Given: Total hang time (t). Gravity (g = 9.8 m/sÂ²).
  • Hint: If the rocket goes up and comes down in total time t, how long did it take to reach the peak?
  • Equation: Use a kinematic equation for displacement (Δy) where vtop = 0.
  • y = ½ g (tup)2

2. Initial Velocity (v0)

Goal: Find the speed of the rocket the moment it left the launch pad.

  • Hint: Use the time it took to reach the peak (tup).
  • Equation: vf = v0 + at
  • At the peak, velocity is zero. Solve for v0.

3. Thrust Phase Analysis

Goal: Analyze the short period (approx 0.2s) where water is ejecting.

  • Thrust Time (tthrust): Usually ~0.2s to 0.3s.
  • Acceleration during thrust: How quickly did it go from 0 to v0 (calculated in step 2)?
  • Force of Thrust: Use Newton's 2nd Law (F=ma). Remember gravity is also acting on the rocket!

4. Final Velocity (vf)

Goal: How fast was it going when it hit the ground?

  • Hint: Symmetry of freefall. If it starts at v0 and lands at the same height, what is vf?
  • Check your signs! Velocity downward is negative.
