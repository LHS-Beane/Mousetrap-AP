Unit 1: Kinematics

Describing motion in one and two dimensions using multiple representations: graphs, vectors, diagrams, and mathematical models.

AP Physics 1 Alignment

Big Ideas: Systems, Force Interactions, Change, Conservation

Science Practices: Representations (SP1), Mathematical Routines (SP2), Argumentation (SP6), Data Analysis (SP5), Experimental Methods (SP3)

Core Representations of Motion

1. Position vs Time ($x$–$t$)

Shows where an object is at each moment.

  • Slope = velocity
  • Curved graph → changing velocity (acceleration)
  • Flat line → object at rest
2. Velocity vs Time ($v$–$t$)

One of the most tested graph types.

  • Slope = acceleration
  • Area under curve = displacement
  • Line below time axis → motion in negative direction
Motion Map Dots
3. Motion Maps

Dots show the object's position at equal time intervals.

  • Equal spacing → constant velocity
  • Increasing spacing → speeding up
  • Decreasing spacing → slowing down
Vector Components
4. Vectors & Components

Displacement and velocity are vectors. Distance and speed are scalars.

Break a vector into components:

$v_x = v\cos\theta$    $v_y = v\sin\theta$

5. Projectile Motion

Horizontal and vertical motions are independent.

  • $a_x = 0$ (no air resistance)
  • $a_y = -g$ downward
  • Time of flight controlled by vertical motion
Reference Frames
6. Relative Motion & Reference Frames

Velocity depends on the observer's frame.

Example relationship:

$\vec{v}_{A/B} = \vec{v}_{A/E} - \vec{v}_{B/E}$

Constant-Acceleration Kinematics

These equations only apply when acceleration is constant.

1. Velocity–Time

$$v = v_0 + at$$

Use when you know acceleration and time.

2. Position–Time

$$x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$

Use when you know displacement over time.

3. Velocity–Position

$$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)$$

Use when time is not given.

Quick Check (Self-Quiz):

  • Can you explain what each symbol means (units + direction)?
  • Can you decide which equation to use based on the givens?
  • Can you sketch a $v$–$t$ graph for an object that starts at rest and speeds up?

Example: Car Accelerating from Rest

A car starts from rest and accelerates at $2.0\ \text{m/s}^2$ for $5.0\ \text{s}$.

  • Find the final velocity.
  • Find how far the car travels.
  • Sketch the $v$–$t$ graph and shade the area under the graph. How does this area compare to your displacement?

(You can turn this into a worked example or leave it as practice.)

Unit 1 Labs & Investigations

1. Constant Velocity Buggy Lab

Objective: Develop a mathematical model for constant velocity motion using experimental data.

Data Collection: Measure position of the buggy at equal time intervals.

  • Plot $x$ (vertical) vs. $t$ (horizontal).
  • Fit a best-fit line: $x = mt + b$.
  • Interpret slope ($m$) as velocity and intercept ($b$) as initial position.

CER Prompt: Write a Claim–Evidence–Reasoning paragraph answering: “Is the buggy truly moving at a constant velocity?”

2. Cart on a Ramp (Uniform Acceleration)

Objective: Use motion data to determine acceleration down an incline and connect to kinematic equations.

  • Measure position at different times as the cart rolls down.
  • Plot $x$ vs. $t$ (curve expected).
  • Linearize by plotting $x$ vs. $t^2$.
  • Slope of $x$ vs. $t^2$ graph $\approx \frac{1}{2}a$.

Extensions: Compare $a$ from graph to $a$ predicted from $a = g\sin\theta$.

3. Projectile Motion Video / Tracker Lab

Objective: Analyze a 2D projectile’s motion and show independence of horizontal and vertical components.

  • Use video analysis or frame-by-frame screenshots of a launched projectile.
  • Create $x$ vs. $t$ and $y$ vs. $t$ graphs.
  • Show that horizontal velocity is (approximately) constant.
  • Show that vertical motion has constant acceleration downward.

CER Prompt: “Does the experimental data support the model that horizontal and vertical motions are independent?”
